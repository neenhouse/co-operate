# CoOperate Vision

> A capital and coordination network for worker-owned businesses — not a
> cooperative CMS. The features in `prd.md` are the free bundled layer. The
> business is the network, the capital pipe, and the trust collateral.

## The one-line thesis

As AI makes production free and forgery trivial, verified collective trust
becomes a premium asset. CoOperate is the trust manufacturing system for
worker-owned cooperatives — it makes forming and running a co-op cheaper,
safer, and better-capitalized, and produces verifiable democratic provenance
as the primary output, not a byproduct.

## The irreducible human need

Worker ownership solves a problem that gets *worse* as AI advances: **who
captures the value when production costs collapse to zero?**

In a conventional business, AI-driven productivity gains flow to owners.
Workers become more productive but don't capture the surplus — the same
dynamic that has driven wealth concentration for decades, now accelerating.
Worker ownership is the structural answer: the people who do the work own
the enterprise, so productivity gains flow to them by design.

But collective ownership requires something AI cannot provide: **collective
governance under real stakes.** Fifteen people must agree on how to split
surplus, how to handle a member who isn't contributing, whether to take on
debt, when to reinvest vs. distribute. These are not information problems
or optimization problems. They are *commitment problems* — agreeing to a
structure before you know how it will play out, then living with the
consequences when votes don't go your way.

AI can draft the bylaws, model the scenarios, and generate the documents.
It cannot:
- Make a group of workers trust each other enough to share ownership
- Build the interpersonal capital that prevents exit when the first hard
  vote goes against you
- Resolve the emotional weight of a founding member being asked to leave
- Create the shared identity that makes a cooperative more than a corporate
  structure with unusual paperwork
- Generate the *social proof* that a specific group of humans successfully
  governed themselves over time

These are irreducibly human because they require *commitment under
uncertainty* — and the verified record of that commitment is what
appreciates in value as AI makes everything else cheaper to fake.

CoOperate's role: make the process of collective governance cheaper, safer,
and better-capitalized so more groups of workers can attempt it. Then
capture the verified output of that process (trust collateral) as the
durable asset. The software is the factory. The trust is the product.

## Audience

- **Retiring owners without succession plans** — 2.9M US small businesses owned
  by people 55+. Most have no buyer. Worker conversion is a real exit. *This is
  the wedge audience.*
- **Workers converting an existing business** — The counterparty to #1. Labor
  ready to buy the thing they already run.
- **New worker co-op founders** — People launching from zero, intimidated by
  legal, capital, and governance complexity.
- **Existing co-ops scaling up** — Cooperatives that outgrew spreadsheets and
  need real infrastructure.

Policy advocates and cooperative federations (USFWC, DAWI, Project Equity) are
partners, not a standalone audience.

## Why this is a real business, not a vitamin

The weakest assumption in most cooperative-tech pitches is *"the binding
constraint on co-op formation is software."* It isn't. In rough order, the
binding constraints are:

1. **Capital access** — banks don't underwrite worker-owned LLCs well
2. **Founder equity psychology** — why take 1/7th when VC offers 30%?
3. **Customer acquisition** for the cooperative itself
4. **Legal recognition** that varies state by state
5. **Dispute resolution** when democracy deadlocks
6. …software is #6 at best

A governance UI plus legal templates plus a dashboard is a vitamin. Bundled
with capital access and shared services, it becomes the aspirin. CoOperate
solves constraints 1, 4, and 5 as the business, and gives away 6 as the
substrate.

## The wedge — how CoOperate makes money

Three layers, priced so the platform *pays* co-ops to be on it.

1. **Capital facilitation fee** — CoOperate is not a loan broker; it's a
   cooperative credit bureau. Standardized, verified financials and
   governance data de-risk underwriting for cooperative lenders (Shared
   Capital Cooperative, Seed Commons, Start.coop, CDFIs, state co-op
   development funds, ESOP-adjacent lenders). The fee is for reducing the
   lender's cost of due diligence, not for origination. This is closer to
   Plaid than to LendingTree. Revenue model: percentage of capital deployed,
   paid by the lender as an underwriting cost reduction.

   Revenue ceiling reality check: $500M–$1B annual cooperative capital ×
   1–3% = $5M–$30M. Enough to bootstrap and sustain, not enough to be the
   permanent primary revenue line. This layer is the *acquisition engine*
   (co-ops come for the capital), not the *retention engine*.

   **Capital expansion thesis — the bigger play.** The $500M–$1B ceiling
   assumes CoOperate only serves *existing* cooperative-friendly lenders
   (CDFIs, Shared Capital, Seed Commons). These are mission-driven, low-
   volume, relationship-based institutions — maybe 20-30 nationally. They
   already know the intermediaries personally and do bespoke underwriting.
   CoOperate's standardized data package saves them time but doesn't
   fundamentally change their capacity.

   The bigger thesis: CoOperate makes cooperative lending *legible to
   mainstream lenders* — community banks, SBA lenders, credit unions — who
   currently don't touch worker buyouts because they can't evaluate the
   structure. If CoOperate's trust collateral translates cooperative risk
   into language that conventional underwriting models accept, the
   addressable capital pool jumps from $1B to $50B+ (SBA alone deploys
   ~$28B annually). The Plaid analogy works here: Plaid didn't optimize
   existing bank-to-bank data flows, it made consumer financial data
   readable by a new class of lenders (fintechs). CoOperate's version:
   make cooperative governance and financial data readable by a new class
   of lenders (SBA, community banks).

   **Resolved:** Start with CDFI optimization, architect for mainstream
   expansion. CDFIs are the proving ground — they already want to lend to
   co-ops and will adopt a cooperative credit signal first. Once CoOperate-
   verified co-ops show measurably lower default rates (3-5 years of data),
   that evidence makes the signal legible to mainstream lenders. The data
   model and verification infrastructure support mainstream lender
   requirements from day one, but go-to-market is CDFI-first. See
   strategic decision #6 for full rationale.

2. **Shared services as a meta-cooperative** — CoOperate operates as a
   purchasing cooperative for cooperatives. Bookkeeping, compliance,
   insurance, payroll, bonding, legal — procured at network scale through
   group negotiation, not resold at markup. This is deeply aligned with
   cooperative Principle 6 (cooperation among cooperatives) and has strong
   precedent (NCBA CLUSA, healthcare purchasing co-ops). The co-op saves
   real OPEX; CoOperate takes a slice of the procurement savings. This
   becomes the primary revenue driver at scale, where group purchasing
   power creates meaningful margin. This is the retention engine.

   **Revenue math stress test.** 200 co-ops × 15 members (US median) =
   3,000 workers across 40 states and 20 industries. Group purchasing
   power for 3,000 dispersed workers is negligible — insurance carriers,
   payroll providers, and bookkeeping firms already serve small businesses
   at competitive rates. Purchasing cooperatives that actually work (Ace
   Hardware, healthcare purchasing co-ops) succeed through *vertical
   density* — thousands of similar businesses with overlapping procurement.
   The boomer succession wedge explicitly produces *horizontal diversity*,
   which is what purchasing cooperatives struggle with.

   The honest number is probably 500–1,000 co-ops with vertical clustering
   before shared services generate meaningful savings. This means the
   revenue transition from capital facilitation to shared services may not
   happen until year 6-8, not year 3-4. Bridging strategies: (a) focus
   the wedge on 1-2 industries with natural density (home care, childcare
   — both have municipal conversion funding), (b) identify a third revenue
   layer that bridges the gap (consulting/advisory, data licensing,
   government contracts), or (c) accept a longer ramp and capitalize
   accordingly.

3. **Operations SaaS** — governance, profit share, member management, legal
   templates. Bundled free above a capital/services threshold. Table stakes,
   not product. Exists to generate the governance data and trust collateral
   that feed layers 1 and 2.

Per-seat SaaS pricing is explicitly rejected. Co-ops are small (US median
worker co-op is ~15 people), margin-sensitive, and will churn on price. The
model has to be aligned, not extractive.

### Who pays what — the money flow

| Payer | What they pay for | When | Amount |
|-------|-------------------|------|--------|
| CDFI / cooperative lender | Reduced due diligence cost (capital facilitation fee) | Per funded deal | 1-3% of deployed capital |
| Government / EDA / state programs | Technical infrastructure for funded conversion programs | Per contract | $50K-$500K per program |
| Member co-ops (at scale) | Shared services procurement savings | Monthly, after ~200 co-ops | Slice of group purchasing delta |
| Intermediaries | Advisory + custom conversion support (bridge only) | Per engagement | $5K-$25K per conversion |

**What no one pays for:** The governance SaaS. Per-seat pricing is
structurally wrong for 15-person cooperatives with thin margins. The SaaS
is the factory; the trust collateral is the product. Charging the factory
floor for existing is backwards.

**Unit economics sketch at 200 co-ops (year 3-4):**
- Average conversion capital deployed: $500K-$2M
- Capital facilitation fee (2%): $10K-$40K per funded deal
- ~50 funded deals/year at steady state: $500K-$2M annual capital revenue
- Government contracts: $200K-$500K annual
- Advisory fees: $100K-$200K annual
- **Total: $800K-$2.7M** — sustains a 5-8 person team on patient capital,
  does not satisfy venture returns. This is by design.

### Channel strategy — B2B2C, not B2C

CoOperate acquires co-ops through the intermediaries who run conversions:
cooperative development organizations (Project Equity, ICA Group, Democracy
at Work Institute), attorneys specializing in cooperative law, SBDC advisors,
and state cooperative development centers. The product for intermediaries is
a conversion management platform — deal pipeline, document generation,
compliance tracking, capital matching. The product for workers is the
post-conversion operating system — governance, profit sharing, member
management. Intermediaries are the acquisition channel; workers are the
retention surface. This resolves the mismatch between the wedge audience
(B2B2C) and the product features (B2C) — both are necessary, but
intermediary tooling comes first in the build order.

**Intermediary capacity is the real bottleneck.** The channel is small:
Project Equity (~15-20 staff), ICA Group (~10-15), DAWI (~5-10), state
cooperative development centers (~30-40 people nationally), independent
cooperative attorneys (~20-30), SBDC advisors with co-op experience (~50).
Total: ~130-165 people. Each manages 2-5 active conversions. National
annual conversion capacity is probably 200-400 deals. At a 12-36 month
cycle, that's ~100-200 new co-ops per year at peak adoption.

CoOperate's growth is therefore gated by intermediary throughput, not by
demand or product quality. The implication: CoOperate's role is not just
to *serve* intermediaries but to *scale their capacity*. If the conversion
toolkit makes a cooperative developer who handles 3 deals handle 10 (via
AI-augmented document generation, compliance automation, capital matching),
the channel grows 3×. This reframes Tier 2 from a CRM into an
AI-augmented conversion accelerator — the difference between digitizing a
workflow and expanding it.

## The moat — four layers, in order of defensibility

### 1. Trust collateral — cooperative credit signal (the primary moat)
CoOperate-verified financials, governance health, and member engagement
become a third-party credit signal — FICO for cooperatives. Lenders price
against it; municipalities accept it for set-aside qualification; buyers
recognize it. Trust accrues through repeated verified outcomes over years,
not through generation. This is the hardest moat to replicate because it
requires *the same history of verified outcomes*, and history cannot be
back-dated.

Why this is #1: every other moat can theoretically be built by a
well-funded competitor starting today. Trust collateral cannot, because it
is a function of time × verified outcomes. The longer CoOperate operates,
the wider the gap. A new entrant in year 3 faces a 3-year trust deficit
that no amount of capital can close.

### 2. Longitudinal democratic governance outcomes dataset
The only dataset on earth linking governance patterns (quorum rules, voting
thresholds, board structures, surplus distribution formulas) to cooperative
survival at 3/5/10 years. First-mover permanent: you cannot back-fill a
decade of outcomes. This data also feeds back into the trust collateral
layer — lenders who see that CoOperate-recommended governance structures
have 2× survival rates will price accordingly.

### 3. Living regulatory graph (outcome-annotated)
Real-time jurisdictional data: which states recognize worker co-op LLCs,
Section 1042 rollover eligibility, patronage dividend tax treatment, state
set-aside eligibility, conversion case law. AI can generate a raw
regulatory database. What it cannot replicate is the *outcome annotations*:
which state rules, combined with which governance structures, led to which
cooperative outcomes. The graph is defensible because it's annotated with
layer 2 data.

### 4. Inter-cooperative trade network (real but late-stage)
Member co-ops trade with each other on preferential terms. A new co-op
joining CoOperate gets instant access to B2B customers and suppliers already
on the platform. This is a classic network effect — every new co-op makes
CoOperate more valuable to the next one.

Honest constraint: this moat has the hardest cold-start problem. The boomer
succession wedge produces co-ops across diverse industries and geographies;
a converted bakery in Portland and a converted plumber in Atlanta don't
trade with each other. Trade networks require supply-chain density within
industries, not just volume. This moat becomes real at 500+ co-ops with
vertical clustering — likely year 4-5, not year 1-2. Until then, the trust
collateral and governance data are the working moats.

## The flywheel — how the moat compounds

The four moat layers are not independent defenses. They form a reinforcing
loop where each layer's output feeds the next layer's input:

```
    More co-ops join CoOperate
           │
           ▼
    More governance data generated
    (votes, financials, member engagement)
           │
           ▼
    Survival predictions sharpen
    ("co-ops with structure X survive 2× longer")
           │
           ▼
    Credit signal strengthens
    (lenders see measurably lower default rates)
           │
           ▼
    Capital costs decrease for verified co-ops
    (lower rates, faster approval, higher approval %)
           │
           ▼
    More co-ops join CoOperate  ◄────────────────┐
    (platform lowers their cost of capital)       │
           │                                      │
           └──────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The acceleration mechanism:** Each primary loop also widens three
secondary flywheels:

- **Shared services flywheel:** More member co-ops → better group
  purchasing rates → higher per-co-op OPEX savings → stronger retention
  → more member co-ops.
- **Intermediary capacity flywheel:** More conversions through the platform
  → better conversion tools (trained on real pipeline data) → higher
  intermediary throughput → more conversions.
- **Regulatory coverage flywheel:** More co-ops across more states → more
  jurisdictional outcome data → better compliance automation → lower
  conversion friction in new states → more co-ops in those states.

**Why the flywheel is defensible:** A competitor entering in year 3 cannot
spin up the flywheel by spending money. They need *years of verified
governance outcomes across hundreds of cooperatives* — and that requires
the cooperatives to already be on their platform, which requires the credit
signal to already be strong, which requires the governance data to already
exist. The flywheel's cold-start problem is also its moat: once spinning,
the energy required to start a competing flywheel exceeds the energy to
sustain this one.

**Flywheel kill metrics** — the numbers that prove the flywheel is spinning:

| Metric | Threshold | What it proves |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Default rate differential | CoOperate-verified co-ops default ≤50% of baseline | Credit signal is real, not decorative |
| Capital cost reduction | Verified co-ops get ≥100bps lower rate | Lenders price against the signal |
| Intermediary throughput | 3× deals per intermediary vs. pre-CoOperate | Capacity multiplier works |
| Retention at 3 years | ≥80% of co-ops still active on platform | Post-conversion stickiness |
| Shared services adoption | ≥40% of member co-ops use ≥1 shared service | Retention engine is firing |

If these metrics don't move within their respective time horizons, the
flywheel thesis is wrong and CoOperate is a nice governance tool with a
vitamin business model. Honest measurement matters more than optimism.

## The AI-future thesis — offensive, not defensive

Most "AI durability" statements are defensive: *"AI augments, humans decide."*
That's correct but uninteresting. The offensive argument is what makes
CoOperate irreplaceable when AI commoditizes software generation.

### The core insight: AI makes building trivial but trust scarce

AI collapses the cost of *production* to near zero. But it simultaneously
collapses the cost of *forgery* to near zero. When every document could be
synthetic, every review fabricated, and every credential generated, the things
that retain value are those with *verifiable provenance* — evidence that real
humans made real decisions with real stakes.

CoOperate doesn't just record decisions; it creates a **chain of democratic
custody** — every proposal, discussion, amendment, vote, and outcome is
immutably linked to identified human participants. This chain is a form of
organizational identity that appreciates as synthetic identity proliferates.

**CoOperate is a trust manufacturing system.** The governance features are the
input. The verified democratic provenance is the output. The SaaS is the
factory. The trust signal is the product. Trust is the one thing AI cannot
manufacture — it can only forge, which makes the real thing more valuable.

### The verification gap — who verifies the verifier?

The chain of democratic custody is only as strong as its verification
mechanism. Right now, the trust collateral is CoOperate self-attesting:
"We recorded that 12 people voted on this proposal." Self-attestation is
no more trustworthy than a company self-reporting ESG metrics — and the
document explicitly fears the B Corp failure mode (ethical badge, not
credit signal).

For trust collateral to be a *credit signal* rather than marketing, it
needs a verification layer independent of CoOperate's platform integrity.
Options, in order of feasibility:

1. **Cross-validation against external records.** Match governance
   decisions against bank records, state filings, tax returns. Verified
   data is stronger because it triangulates multiple sources. Lowest
   friction, highest credibility.
2. **Lender participation in verification.** Lenders who price against
   the data also validate it, creating a self-reinforcing credibility
   loop. Aligns incentives — lenders already do due diligence; CoOperate
   structures and standardizes it.
3. **Third-party audit protocol.** Periodic independent audits of
   governance data (analogous to financial audits). Real credibility but
   adds cost and friction.
4. **Cryptographic attestation.** Members sign votes with personal keys;
   the chain is mathematically verifiable without trusting CoOperate's
   database. Technically elegant but adds UX friction to a 15-person
   bakery co-op.

**Resolved:** Cross-validation (matching governance records against bank
transactions, state filings, and tax records) is the verification
foundation. Lender participation (structuring CDFI due diligence as a
verification event that feeds back into the trust collateral) is the
feedback loop. Third-party audits deferred to year 3+. Cryptographic
attestation rejected — UX burden contradicts "professional simplicity"
for a 15-person cooperative. See strategic decision #7 for full
rationale. This shapes the Tier 1 data model: every governance action
must be linkable to external financial records from day one.

### Four forces

1. **AI collapses the solo-founder moat.** A one-person SaaS gets cloned in a
   weekend. Workers will need collective leverage or they get commoditized
   with the software. Worker ownership is the counter-gravity. (But see
   anti-thesis #5 — this is the biggest macro bet.)
2. **AI devalues unverified content, which makes verified content premium.**
   This is not an ethical argument; it's a market argument. Provenance sold
   as an ethical badge is worthless (the B Corp lesson). Provenance sold as
   a *credit signal* — verified financials, verified governance, verified
   member engagement — is worth real money because it lowers the cost of
   capital. Lenders price against risk; verified provenance reduces risk;
   CoOperate captures the spread.
3. **Coordination remains the scarce skill.** Building is nearly free; aligning
   a group around a shared objective is not. CoOperate sells coordination
   infrastructure, not document infrastructure. But the stronger framing:
   CoOperate sells the *verified output of coordination* (trust collateral),
   not the coordination process itself.
4. **Policy tailwind accelerates.** AI-driven labor dislocation, wealth
   concentration, and the boomer succession wave make worker ownership a
   policy target in multiple jurisdictions. CoOperate becomes subsidized
   public-benefit infrastructure, not a discretionary purchase. The trust
   collateral layer makes CoOperate legible to policymakers: "co-ops on
   CoOperate have 2× survival rates" is a fundable claim.

### Durability test

*"If the next generation of LLMs makes every feature in `prd.md` trivially
generatable, does CoOperate still matter?"*

Under the original vision — no. Under this thesis — yes, because the product
is the trust collateral, the governance data, and the capital facilitation
pipe. None are generatable. The SaaS features are the substrate that produces
these assets, and making the substrate cheaper (via AI) makes the assets
*more* valuable — exactly the asymmetry CoOperate needs.

### Competitive landscape — who tries in 5 years, and why they fail

When AI gives every competitor the same building blocks, the question is
not "can they build this?" but "can they spin up the flywheel?" Five
archetypes will try:

**1. AI-powered legal generators (LegalZoom + Claude, Stripe Atlas for
co-ops).** They build cooperative formation documents in 15 minutes. They
fail because documents are the easiest part — CoOperate gives them away
free. The hard part is what happens *after* formation: ongoing governance,
verified outcomes, capital access. A document generator has no post-
formation relationship and therefore no data, no credit signal, and no
flywheel.

**2. Fintech cooperative lending platform (a new Plaid-for-cooperatives).**
They build a lending marketplace matching co-ops to capital. They fail
because without verified governance data, they're matchmakers without a
credit signal. They can connect co-ops to lenders but cannot reduce the
lender's due diligence cost because they have no verified track record of
cooperative outcomes. Building that track record requires a governance
platform with years of data — which is CoOperate.

**3. Open-source cooperative governance tools.** They replicate every
feature in `prd.md`, available free. They fail because open source
replicates the substrate but not the output. The value is not in the
governance tool — it's in the *aggregated, verified, cross-cooperative
dataset* that the tool produces. A thousand independent installations
generate a thousand isolated datasets. CoOperate's value is the unified
dataset and the network built on top of it.

**4. Government-built cooperative development portals.** State-level
conversion tracking and compliance tools. They fail because government
platforms are jurisdictional. CoOperate's value is cross-jurisdictional:
the credit signal is portable, the regulatory graph spans all states, the
shared services network is national. A state portal in Oregon doesn't help
a co-op in Michigan. Government builds to minimum spec and doesn't iterate.

**5. Existing federations building in-house tools.** USFWC, DAWI, etc.
build internal pipeline management for their own conversions. They fail
because they're 5-15 person organizations whose mission is cooperative
development, not software. More importantly, a federation-specific tool
only has data from that federation's conversions (~50-100 per year).
CoOperate aggregates across *all* federations, producing the cross-network
dataset and credit signal that no single federation can generate alone.

**The common failure: time cannot be purchased.** Every competitor faces
the same constraint: the flywheel requires years of verified governance
outcomes to produce a meaningful credit signal. Capital cannot compress
this timeline. Hiring cannot compress it. AI cannot generate synthetic
governance history that lenders would trust. CoOperate's head start is
permanent and compounding.

### AI's role inside the product

AI drafts proposals, surfaces anomalies, models profit-share scenarios,
translates legal language, pre-fills conversion documents, and flags governance
patterns correlated with failure. It never holds a binding vote. The ethical
floor is human-final governance. The business floor is that AI makes the
*features* cheaper and the *trust signal* more valuable.

## Cold-start strategy

The network moat has a classic chicken-and-egg problem: the first 50 co-ops
on CoOperate get no network benefit. The answer is vertical entry, not
horizontal launch.

**Wedge vertical: boomer business succession.** 2.9M US businesses owned by
people 55+, most with no succession plan. Orgs like Project Equity, Democracy
at Work Institute, and ICA Group already fund worker conversions; state and
city governments increasingly subsidize them. CoOperate becomes *the*
conversion toolkit for this tailwind. The market grows for demographic
reasons independent of CoOperate's execution — which is the definition of a
good wedge.

**Secondary verticals** (pursue after wedge proves): home care / childcare
cooperatives (municipally funded conversion), platform-worker cooperatives
(high narrative value, thinner funding base).

## Anti-thesis — where this could fail

Every claim above has a failure mode. Listing them honestly, in order of
how existential the risk is:

1. **Founder equity psychology may not shift.** If AI actually *strengthens*
   the solo-founder advantage instead of eroding it, collective ownership
   stays niche. One person with Claude can now do what ten could before —
   why split ownership ten ways? This is the biggest macro bet. It's
   unhedgeable by product work and unfalsifiable until the market plays out.
   Partial mitigation: the boomer succession wedge doesn't depend on this
   bet — those businesses already exist and already need buyers.

2. **The conversion pipeline has human bottlenecks, not software bottlenecks.**
   A worker buyout involves contested valuation, multi-source financing,
   6–18 months of legal restructuring, novel governance design, and cultural
   transition from employee to owner. Each step involves human intermediaries
   (attorneys, cooperative developers, facilitators) who currently have no
   incentive to route through CoOperate. If the intermediaries don't adopt
   the platform, the wedge doesn't work. Mitigation: the B2B2C channel
   strategy — build the intermediary toolkit first, make their pipeline
   management faster, earn their default recommendation.

3. **The CDFI / cooperative capital pool may be too small.** Total deployable
   cooperative-friendly capital in the US is plausibly $500M–$1B annually —
   enough to bootstrap, not enough to be the permanent primary revenue
   driver. The shared-services meta-cooperative layer must become the
   primary revenue engine by ~200 member co-ops. If co-op adoption is
   slower than projected, the revenue transition may not happen before
   runway runs out.

4. **Trust collateral may not command a measurable premium.** B Corp
   certification didn't command a meaningful premium. If CoOperate
   verification is perceived as an ethical badge rather than a credit
   signal, the primary moat is decorative. Mitigation: sell provenance as
   an *operational input* to underwriting (lower rates), contract
   qualification (set-asides), and insurance pricing (lower risk) — never
   as a badge. The test: does a lender give a CoOperate-verified co-op a
   lower rate? If yes, the moat works. If no, it's marketing.

5. **Vertical entry takes years.** Boomer succession deals close slowly —
   12 to 36 months per conversion. Network effects may take 5+ years to
   compound. Runway and patience requirements are non-trivial. This is a
   venture-incompatible timeline unless the capital facilitation fees
   generate meaningful revenue during the ramp.

6. **AI-enabled DIY may erode the SaaS layer faster than the network forms.**
   If Claude-plus-DocuSign lets a founder replicate `prd.md` in a weekend,
   the bundled-free software layer may never feel valuable enough to drive
   acquisition. Mitigation: acquire through the capital funnel and the
   intermediary channel, not the software funnel. Co-ops don't come for the
   governance tool; they come for the loan and the conversion support.

7. **The trust collateral may be self-attested, not verified.** If CoOperate
   cannot establish an independent verification mechanism for its governance
   data, the "chain of democratic custody" is just a database CoOperate
   controls. Lenders won't price against self-reported data any more than
   they price against self-reported financials. See "The verification gap"
   section under the AI-future thesis. This is solvable but must be solved
   early — the data model and member onboarding need to support verification
   from day one, not retrofit it later.

8. **CoOperate's own capital structure may contradict its thesis.** If
   CoOperate takes VC funding with 3-5 year exit expectations, the
   resulting growth pressure creates incentives to extract from co-ops
   rather than serve them — the exact anti-pattern the platform exists to
   prevent. The 7-10 year timeline to meaningful revenue is venture-
   incompatible. But it may be perfectly compatible with the cooperative
   model itself: patient capital from CDFIs, revenue-based financing,
   EDA cooperative development grants, or structuring CoOperate as a
   platform cooperative owned by its member co-ops. The irony: CoOperate's
   optimal capital structure is cooperative. This isn't just thematically
   appropriate — it's structurally correct, because misaligned capital
   would destroy the trust that is the entire product.

## Design principles

### Trust through transparency
Every decision, vote, and financial allocation is visible to all members.
Exported provenance is a product feature, not a philosophical one.

### Democratic by default
All governance features default to democratic processes: one member, one
vote; proposals require quorum; decisions are recorded permanently. The
platform never shortcuts collective decision-making.

### Professional simplicity
No "alternative economy" aesthetic. CoOperate competes on the same polish
floor as any serious B2B SaaS — because the audience includes lenders,
inspectors, state officials, and acquirers, not just members.

### Human-final governance
AI assists. AI never binds. Every decision with legal, financial, or
membership consequence flows through an explicit human vote.

## Strategic decisions — resolved

Each decision below was surfaced as an open question, analyzed for trade-
offs, and resolved with a recommendation. Rationale is preserved so
future revisions can revisit if assumptions change.

### Previously resolved
- *Is capital origination a sustainable primary revenue line?* **No.** It's
  the acquisition engine ($5M–$30M ceiling). Shared services as a meta-
  cooperative becomes the primary revenue driver at scale.
- *What's the primary moat?* **Trust collateral**, not the trade network.
  The trade network is real but late-stage (year 4-5). Trust collateral
  compounds from day one.
- *B2B or B2C?* **B2B2C.** Intermediary toolkit acquires co-ops; post-
  conversion operating system retains them.

### 1. Build the capital pool or stay facilitation-only?

**Decision: Facilitation-only.**

| | Build lending arm | Facilitation platform |
|---|---|---|
| Pros | Captures more value chain, controls capital flow, stronger lock-in | Capital-light, faster to launch, lower regulatory burden, aligns with verification thesis |
| Cons | Requires financial licenses (state money-lender, potential CDFI certification), enormous capital requirements, mission drift risk, different expertise than software | Revenue dependent on external lenders, less direct control over capital deployment |

CoOperate's moat is *verification*, not capital. Adding a lending arm
dilutes focus, adds regulatory complexity, and is fundamentally a
different business requiring different expertise. The Plaid analogy
holds: Plaid doesn't lend; it makes lending possible. CoOperate
doesn't fund; it makes cooperative funding underwritable. Revisit only
if the trust collateral is proven and lenders still won't adopt it —
at which point CoOperate would have the data to underwrite directly.

### 2. Partner or compete with USFWC / DAWI / Start.coop?

**Decision: Partner as infrastructure provider.**

| | Partner | Compete |
|---|---|---|
| Pros | Instant distribution, credibility by association, aligned with Principle 6, these orgs ARE the intermediary channel | Full product control, faster iteration, no partnership governance overhead |
| Cons | Dependency on partner adoption timelines, potential governance complexity | Antagonizes the only distribution channel (~150 people), loses credibility in a trust-dependent market, tiny market doesn't support two platforms |

Competing against the federations in a market with ~150 intermediaries
is strategic suicide. They *are* the distribution channel. CoOperate
is infrastructure-for-the-federations: make their conversion pipeline
faster, their compliance tracking easier, their capital matching more
efficient. The federations become distribution partners, not
competitors. Specific tactic: co-branding — "Powered by CoOperate" on
conversion tooling that intermediaries already deliver.

### 3. When does the governance dataset become a standalone product?

**Decision: Research partnerships at 200 co-ops (year 3-4). Standalone
data product at 500+ co-ops (year 5-6). No earlier.**

| | Early productization (year 2) | Phased approach |
|---|---|---|
| Pros | Potential revenue diversification, brand building in academic/policy circles | Dataset has statistical significance before monetization, avoids credibility damage from premature claims |
| Cons | Dataset too small to be meaningful, risks publishing noise as signal, diverts product focus | Delays a potential revenue line, slower brand building |

A dataset linking governance structures to cooperative survival needs
enough co-ops, enough time, and enough variance to produce real
findings. At 50 co-ops with 2 years of data, there's no statistical
power — publishing would be noise dressed as insight. At 200+ co-ops
with 3+ years, patterns start emerging. Phase 1: license raw
(anonymized) data to academic researchers (university cooperative
economics programs) for peer-reviewed analysis. This builds
credibility without CoOperate self-publishing. Phase 2: productize
as benchmarking + predictive analytics once the academic work
validates the signal.

### 4. Minimum viable network for inter-coop trade?

**Decision: 500+ co-ops with at least 2 vertical clusters of 50+. Do
not invest in trade infrastructure before this.**

| | Build trade layer early (year 2) | Wait for density (year 5+) |
|---|---|---|
| Pros | Adds immediate differentiation, creates early lock-in | Resources focused on proven moats (trust collateral, governance data), trade network actually works when it launches |
| Cons | No supply-chain density to make it useful, empty marketplace damages credibility | Delays a potential moat, risks competitor pre-empting |

The boomer succession wedge produces geographic and industry
diversity, not vertical density. A converted bakery in Portland and
a converted plumber in Atlanta don't trade with each other.
Purchasing co-op precedents (Ace Hardware, healthcare co-ops) show
that trade value requires ~50+ businesses in a single industry. The
home care / childcare vertical is most likely to cluster first
(municipal conversion funding creates geographic density within one
sector). Build the directory infrastructure passively (member
profiles list industry and capabilities) but don't invest in trade
features or marketplace UX until density justifies it.

### 5. How to acquire the first 10 intermediaries?

**Decision: Free access + co-design partnership.**

| | Pay intermediaries | Subsidize usage | Free + co-design |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pros | Fast adoption | Lower cost than direct payment | Zero cost to intermediary, CoOperate gets real-world design feedback, shared ownership creates advocates |
| Cons | Creates dependency, unsustainable, attracts wrong motivation | Still creates artificial incentive, tool may not stick post-subsidy | Slower, requires trust-building, co-design is time-intensive |

Offer the first 5-10 intermediaries free lifetime access in exchange
for co-designing the conversion pipeline toolkit. They get free tools
AND influence over the product roadmap. CoOperate gets distribution
AND ground-truth design feedback from practitioners. The co-design
cohort becomes the advocacy base — intermediaries who helped build the
tool will recommend it because it reflects their workflow, not because
they were paid to. After the co-design phase (6-12 months), the tool
should demonstrably save enough time (target: 3× throughput
improvement) to drive organic adoption from the remaining ~140
intermediaries.

### 6. Capital expansion vs. capital optimization?

**Decision: Start with CDFI optimization, architect for mainstream
expansion.**

| | CDFI-only | Mainstream expansion from day 1 | Phased approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pros | Faster, simpler, known buyer, mission-aligned | Bigger market, higher ceiling, stronger long-term thesis | Proves the model in friendly territory before the harder sell |
| Cons | $1B ceiling may not sustain the business | Mainstream lenders move slowly, regulatory translation is hard, longer time to first revenue | Requires discipline to not get stuck in phase 1 |

Mainstream lenders (SBA, community banks) won't adopt a cooperative
credit signal until it has a track record. CDFIs will. So CDFIs are
the proving ground, not the permanent market. Build the trust
collateral with CDFI lenders who already want to lend to co-ops.
Once CoOperate-verified co-ops have measurably lower default rates
(3-5 years of data), *that evidence* is what makes the signal legible
to mainstream lenders. The data model and verification infrastructure
should support mainstream lender requirements from day one (standard
financial reporting formats, integration with conventional
underwriting systems), but the go-to-market is CDFI-first. The phase
transition trigger: when CoOperate can show a statistically
significant reduction in default rates for verified co-ops vs.
unverified ones. That's the pitch deck for community banks.

### 7. Verification mechanism?

**Decision: Cross-validation + lender participation (options 1 + 2).
Reject cryptographic attestation.**

| | Cross-validation | Lender participation | Third-party audit | Cryptographic attestation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pros | Low friction, high credibility, triangulates multiple data sources | Aligns incentives, lenders already do diligence, self-reinforcing loop | Highest independent credibility | Mathematically verifiable, trustless |
| Cons | Requires data integrations (banks, state filings), coverage varies by jurisdiction | Requires lender buy-in before trust is proven (chicken-and-egg) | Expensive, adds friction, overkill for early stage | Massive UX friction for a 15-person bakery, key management is a non-starter for target users |

**Layer 1: Cross-validation (foundation).** Connect governance records
to external data: bank transactions (via Plaid or equivalent),
state business filings, tax records. When CoOperate says "this co-op
distributed $50K in surplus per their governance vote," the bank
records confirm $50K actually moved. This is the verification
bedrock — it requires data integrations but zero behavior change from
co-op members.

**Layer 2: Lender participation (feedback loop).** Structure lender due
diligence as a verification event — when a CDFI reviews a CoOperate
co-op's application, their findings feed back into the trust
collateral. Lender diligence is already happening; CoOperate
standardizes and records it. This creates the self-reinforcing loop:
more lending → more verification → stronger signal → more lending.

Third-party audits are deferred to year 3+ when scale justifies the
cost. Cryptographic attestation is rejected — the UX burden
contradicts the "professional simplicity" design principle, and the
target user (a 15-person home care cooperative) will not manage
signing keys.

### 8. Revenue bridge between capital facilitation and shared services?

**Decision: Government contracts + intermediary advisory fees.**

| | Government contracts | Consulting/advisory | Data licensing | Accept lower burn |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pros | Mission-aligned, large pools (EDA, SBA, state co-op dev funds), positions CoOperate as public infrastructure | Direct revenue from existing relationships, low marginal cost | Potential high-margin, unique asset | Simplest, avoids scope creep |
| Cons | Slow procurement cycles, compliance overhead, grant dependency | Doesn't scale, potentially dilutes product focus | Dataset too small during bridge period | Extends time to milestones, increases existential risk |

**Primary bridge: government contracts.** The EDA (Economic
Development Administration) funds cooperative development programs.
State cooperative development centers receive and distribute public
funds. CoOperate can position as technical infrastructure for these
programs — "the platform that municipalities use to manage and track
cooperative conversions they're funding." This is aligned revenue (the
same intermediaries, the same conversions) and positions CoOperate as
public-benefit infrastructure rather than a discretionary SaaS
purchase. Annual EDA cooperative development funding is ~$10-20M;
capturing even a small fraction as a technology platform provides
meaningful bridge revenue.

**Secondary bridge: advisory fees from intermediaries** for custom
conversion support, training, and implementation — but only where this
doesn't conflict with the free co-design partnership in decision #5.
Advisory is labor-intensive and doesn't scale, so it's bridge revenue,
not a business line.

### 9. CoOperate's own capital structure?

**Decision: Public benefit corporation, funded by grants + cooperative-
aligned patient capital. Path to platform cooperative ownership by
member co-ops at scale.**

| | Traditional VC | PBC + grants + patient capital | Platform co-op from day 1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pros | Large capital available, fast scaling, proven playbook | Mission alignment, patient timeline, thesis-consistent, no exit pressure | Ultimate alignment, "eat your own cooking" | 
| Cons | 3-5 year exit pressure, extractive incentives, thesis contradiction | Smaller capital pool, slower scaling, requires creative fundraising | Governance overhead at founding stage, limits early agility, hard to raise any capital |

VC is structurally disqualifying. A fund that needs 10× returns in 5
years will push CoOperate to extract from co-ops (per-seat pricing,
data monetization, predatory capital terms) — exactly the behavior
the platform exists to prevent. A platform cooperative from day 1 is
ideologically pure but practically paralyzing — you can't have member
co-ops governing your product decisions when you have 0 member co-ops.

The PBC structure threads the needle: legal obligation to balance
profit with mission, ability to raise grants and patient capital
(CDFIs, impact investors, EDA programs), and a built-in path to
cooperative governance. Specific plan:

- **Year 0-3:** PBC funded by EDA cooperative development grants,
  CDFI impact investments (revenue-based financing, not equity),
  and program-related investments from cooperative foundations
  (e.g., National Cooperative Bank, CDF Fund).
- **Year 3-5:** As member co-ops reach ~100, introduce an advisory
  board with member co-op representatives. Revenue from capital
  facilitation + government contracts sustains operations.
- **Year 5+:** Convert to multi-stakeholder cooperative with member
  co-ops, intermediaries, and employees as owner classes. This is
  the thesis made structural — CoOperate governed by the ecosystem
  it serves.

### 10. Intermediary throughput: expand capacity or optimize within ceiling?

**Decision: Expand capacity. This is the core product thesis of Tier 2.**

| | Accept ceiling (~200-400/year) | Expand capacity via tooling |
|---|---|---|
| Pros | Simpler product, lower ambition risk | Unlocks growth beyond current bottleneck, positions Tier 2 as a capacity multiplier not a CRM, directly increases TAM |
| Cons | Growth permanently capped at ~200 co-ops/year, may not reach shared services viability | Harder to build, requires deep understanding of intermediary workflows, success metrics are harder to measure |

If CoOperate accepts the ceiling, it never reaches the 500+ co-ops
needed for shared services or trade network viability. The math
doesn't work. So expansion isn't optional — it's existential.

The Tier 2 toolkit must be designed as a **capacity multiplier**:
- AI-augmented document generation (feasibility reports, term sheets,
  compliance filings) reduces per-deal administrative burden by 60-70%
- Automated compliance checklists per jurisdiction eliminate manual
  regulatory research
- Capital matching pre-screening eliminates dead-end lender
  applications
- Template governance structures (pre-tested via the outcomes dataset)
  reduce the governance design phase from weeks to days

Target: an intermediary who currently manages 3 active conversions can
manage 8-10 with CoOperate. This 3× throughput multiplier turns the
~150 intermediary pool from a 200-400/year ceiling into a 600-1,200/
year pipeline. Combined with organic growth in the intermediary pool
itself (CoOperate's training programs + policy tailwinds creating new
cooperative development positions), the realistic year-5 pipeline is
500-800 new co-ops per year — sufficient for shared services viability.

## Remaining open questions

These are genuinely unresolvable without market data and are flagged for
future revisiting as CoOperate operates. Each maps to a flywheel kill
metric and a milestone gate — they are the empirical tests of the thesis.

- **What default rate reduction does CoOperate verification produce?**
  This is the empirical foundation of the trust collateral moat. It
  can only be measured after 3+ years of lending data. If the answer is
  "none," the moat thesis fails regardless of other decisions.
  *Kill metric: default rate differential ≤50% of baseline. Gate: year 3.*

- **Will the home care / childcare vertical cluster fast enough to seed
  the trade network?** This depends on municipal conversion funding
  patterns that are externally driven. Monitor but cannot influence.
  *Kill metric: shared services adoption ≥40%. Gate: year 5.*

- **At what co-op count does the governance outcomes dataset achieve
  statistical significance for survival predictions?** Depends on
  variance in governance structures and outcomes. Likely 200-500 but
  cannot be known until the data exists.
  *Kill metric: academic partnership publishes first survival correlation.
  Gate: year 2-3.*

- **Does the intermediary capacity multiplier actually work?** The 3×
  throughput claim is the foundation of CoOperate's growth thesis — if
  intermediaries using the platform don't convert meaningfully more deals,
  the pipeline ceiling holds and the business never reaches shared services
  viability.
  *Kill metric: intermediary throughput ≥3×. Gate: year 2.*

- **Will lenders price against the cooperative credit signal before the
  dataset is large?** CDFIs are mission-driven and may accept early-stage
  data. But "accept the data package" (year 2 gate) and "price materially
  lower" (year 3 gate) are different thresholds. The revenue model depends
  on the second, not the first.
  *Kill metric: capital cost reduction ≥100bps. Gate: year 3-4.*
